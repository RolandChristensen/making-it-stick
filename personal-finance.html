Notes On Personal Finance

Finance For Everyone: The Basics

Finance is about value. Value creation takes time and has a degree of uncertainty. You make a decision to the best of your ability, knowing you don't know what the future may bring (uncertainty), and then wait (time) to measure the outcome (gather data). Develop a strategy to make good decisions, so the outcome is rarely unpleasant.

Competitive markets enable anybody to profit. There are monopolies, in the real world, but ideally the market is competitive.

Frictions: The cost to start a financial endeavor (fees, taxes, etc..). These should not be so excessive or prohibitive that you cannot overcome them given time and your good ideas should be able to be acted upon.

Capital should be able to flow relatively easily. Artificial barriers, such as interest rates are designed to control the flow when natural flow starts to harm the economy.

Time Value of Money

The future is made up of decisions. Important decisions affect us more significantly and potentially for longer.

Deeply understand the impact of just the passage of time on a decision before we make it.

Try to assess the risk, but this is an advanced topic that will not be covered here. Seek out another course to understand that more deeply.

Time Lines & Convention

  • PV = Present Value ($)
  • FV = Future Value ($)
  • n = # of Periods (#): year, month, etc..
  • r = Interest Rate (%) > 0 (Assume positive)
  • Risk: Assumes higher rate of return

Rate of Return

  • initial value: 100
  • period of time: 1 month
  • final value: 102
  • rate of return: final value - initial value / initial value = 2%

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow.

Future Value (Annuity)

This simply represents our abiity to invest and get interest on that investment.
Future Value = Initial Amount + Accumulated Interest
FV = P + r*P = (1 + r)P
Future Value Factor (FVF) = 1 + r = The value of one dollar one period from now

Multiple Periods (Compounding)

Future Value = P(1+r)(1+r) = P(1+r)^n
n = the number of periods

Excel and Google Sheets has a FV function you can use to calculate future value
The number generated will be negative or positive based on the PV entered and depends on your perspective (debits & credits)

Monthly compounding on a yearly interest rate:
FV = P(1 + r/12)^n

Present Value (Discounting)

This represents the idea that $100 today is worth more than $100 in the future.
Inflation rate is the first example of this
Also, the fact that investing that same money would yield a rate of return rather than simply sitting under your mattress.
Think of this as the present value of money sitting under your mattress 'n' years from now.
PV = FV / 1 + r
Multiple Periods: PV = FV / (1 + r)^2
Discounting: The act of calculating initial investment

Excel has function for discounting

FV of an Annuity: Formula
FVAnnuity=C(1+r)2+C(1+r)+C
= C[(1+r)2+(1+r)+1]
= C * Future Value Annuity Factor (FVAF): composed of the rate (r) and the number of periods (n)

The difference between this and the other FV, is that we get multiple cash flows.

Saving Regularly: FV of an Annuity

What will be the value of your bank account if you deposit $1,000 every year in a bank. You plan to leave homme in five years and expect to earn 5% in your bank account?

Normally, we skip t=0 and assume we start tracking at the end of the first period, t=1.

=FV(0.05, 5, -1000)

To make this more clear, we are losing $1000 every period to the bank. We can make the PMT negative, which makes sense or negate the entire equation to get the cash flow correct for us at the end. We will be getting the FV calculation. Note: Excel displays negatives with parenthesis around them and is a common notation for negative values from our perspective (The bank sees it as a positive value).

Power of Saving Regularly

How much will be in your bank account 50 years from now if you save $1,000 every year at 5% versus 15%?

The difference is about 7 million dollars. Each percent increase is extremely valuable over a long period!

Saving for the Future: Example 1

You are starting college. How much money do you need in the bank today so that you can spend $3,000 every year for the next four years, starting at the end of this year? Suppose the bank pays an interest rate of 5%.
If you want to have $3000 spending cash each year, how much do you need in the bank right now? This could also apply to how much you need in a retirement account.
=PV(0.05,4,3000)
Double checking your work, by taking it year by year is a good practice to make sure you are doing it right.

Perpetuities

  • A perpetuity is simply a set of equal payments that are paid forever, with or without growth
  • PVperpetuity=C/(1+r)+C/(1+r)^2+...+infinity=C/r
  • Applicable to stocks, something that does not have definite life. Unlike loans or CDs which have a definite period.
  • You are giving them money now hoping that you will get many back (dividends) forever.

Examples of Perpetuities:

  • Stocks
  • Company
  • Long Term Transactions: You can approximate a perpetuity by using a 30 year time frame. =PV(rate,30,payment). This is a very close approximation. The higher the time frame and the higher the interest rate the closer an approximation it is.

Growth Stock: PV=C+C(1+g)+C(1+g)^2+...+infinity
C1+C2+C3+...+infinity
c2=C1(1+g) = C1(1+0.02) if g = 2% growth.
For example if inflation is 2%, you want your growth rate to be 2%.
Growth Rate: C1/(r-g) Doesn't really make sense if g is equal or greater than r. So we use this short hand only when it is applicable and use the long form when it is not.

When trying to predict the future, you are going to be wrong anyway, so you need to try your best to make a decision today that is impacting you forever. Effective Annual Interest Rates

The stated rate is 6%. If you are compounding yearly then it is actually 6%, but if you are compounding interval is one month the interest will be more or less than 6%.
EAR = (1+r/k)^k - 1
where 'k' is the 'compounding interval' or the number of periods in a year and r is the annual interest rate (APR).
EAR = (1+0.06/12)^12 - 1

Emilia's portfolio of savings is making 5% annually. How much does Emilia need to have set aside today/now to pay for four years of college for Jessica?
The instructor recommends always drawing a time line first, with now or 0 on one side and the number of years in advance we are trying to predict.
In these kinds of investment problems there will be a period of investments before cash flows begin.
In a business, you will invest in supplies in advance. In this case you will save for college in advance and begin payments four years before the completion of university.

A simple algorithm

  1. Draw a timeline. You can use a spreadsheet as a natural timeline. Column 'A' being time 0, 'B' being time 1, etc...
  2. Break the problem into bite-size pieces
  3. Solve the pieces in logical order
  4. Use financial tools to help you

The equation is tuition(1+rate of increase)^number of years
=10000(1.025)^n
The Future Value of the tuition, due to inflation, is not necessarily and hopefully not identical to the discount rate.
The Discount Rate is distinct from the Future Value and is suppossed to capture inflation.
Remember that here the cash flow happens ahead of the year in question. We pay tuition before taking classes.

What are Bonds?

Bond: a contract between a borrower and a lender, where the borrower gets a bond (IOU with a promise to pay back the lender at a later date). You can also think of it as a loan.
In general, the risk of losing money on a bond is lower, so the rate of return is low.
There are exceptions. Junk Bonds are called that due to the risk involved, but they offer high returns. Governments fund their activities through bonds for better or for worse.
Stocks are freely traded in the marketplace all over the world and are a way a company can borrow at the cost of paying a higher rate of return on success.
Governments say they will give you say $100000 in a year for a bond. The market talks it over and decides on the price of the bond, which is less than the $100000, say $900000. So you make $10000, for loaning them $90000, a year from now.
Companies also borrow a lot from private banks.
Although mortgages can be lumped together into a large tradable security, which is traded.

Structure of Bonds
State governments issue bonds (municipal bonds), which are publically priced, which allows you to compare to other investments.
Bonds are paid out in two ways:

  1. A Coupon: paid out periodically, decided by the borrower or issuer of the bond. The will pay coupons every year or some other period.
  2. Face value: typically smaller amounts periodically and then one ballon payment at the end of the face value.

Bonds are named such that they have a limited lifetime (maturity). It doesn't go on forever.
There are rare exceptions of that called 'perpetuities'

Draw a timeline with 0 through 10 periods.
Each period is a payment (C for coupon) and the last period gives you a coupon + Face Value (F), paid by the borrower (government).
At 0 period is when the lender pays for the bond (x).
x = p = PV(C's, F)
The time value of money again.

Government Bonds: Zero Coupon
Treasury security: Offered by a governemnt backed by the treasury. A zero coupon bond.
Zero coupon bonds do not pay any coupons, just a Face Value when they mature.
Treasury bill vs. strip: a strip is a zero coupon bond with a maturity greater than one year. A treasury bill is issue with a maturity up to a year.
Price of a zero coupon bond: P = (Face Value) * PF(r,n) = Face Value / (1 + r)^n

Suppose a zero-coupon bond pays $100,000 in exactly one year. What is the price of value/price of the bond today if the interst rate on similar bonds is 5%?

The discount rate is 5%
=PV(0.05, 1, 0, 100000) = 95,238.10
Zero Coupon Bond is also called a Discount Bond.
Discount because the present value must be less than the face value.
Note that we have not taken inflation into the picture, which is a risk with a low interest rate.

Yield to Maturity
The return built into the pricing of the bond is called Yield-to-Maturity (YTM)
=RATE(number_of_periods, payment_per_period, present_value, [future_value], [end_or_beginning], [rate_guess])
for zero coupon bonds = RATE(number_of_periods, 0, present_value, -future_value)
There are no payments and the future value will be negative
The number_of_periods is equal to the compounding interval. Usually this is determined at six months
So a five year bond would have 10 periods of compounding
The coupons (payments) must also be divided by two, since you are getting two payments a year, but the coupon will be stated as a yearly coupon.

Coupon Paying Bonds
Suppose a government bond has a 3% coupon, face value of $100000, and 6 years to maturity. What is the price of this bond given that similar bonds yield an annual return of 4%?
Another timeline is drawn, where the right side is the Face Value + Coupon (C) / 2, since it is compounded every six months and Coupon is stated annually. Interest rates are stated annually, determined by the market. The coupon is determined by the issuer of the bond.
What is the payment being made? What is the coupon: 3% of 100000 = 3000
There are 12 compounding intervals over 6 years
The total coupons equal c/2 * 12
The price equals the PV = PV(rate/2, periods * 2, -payment / 2, FV)
rate = 4% / 2
number of periods = 6 years * 2
Payment = -3000 / 2
Future Value (Face Value) = 100000
=PV(.04/2, 6*2, -3000/2, -100000)
